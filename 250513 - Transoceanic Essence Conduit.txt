**Promethium Technology - Transoceanic Essence Conduits**

Not long after the commencement of large-scale production in the mid-900s, promethium was found to be a conductor of hydrothaumic essence among the Adepts. Numerous investigations of its use in this fashion were undertaken, but yielded little that could not be achieved more cheaply and consistently with fluid flows - that is, save the case of the transoceanic essence conduit.

Starting from the year 928, formal proposals for joining the Siayeo continents via submarine pipelines were recognised, but either failed to acquire funding, were abandoned, or collapsed catastrophically - the latter typically due to the treacherous currents of the Yunamer and Ineng passages. After much effort and expenditure the concept was abandoned, and it was not until long after the laying of submarine telegraph cables in 955 and 961 that promethium was first considered as a means to revive the concept.

Promethium cables were first employed in the Yeoxli Southern Expedition of 978 in a daring forward deployment of hydrothaumic artillery. However, the expenditure was considered great and extremely wasteful, with much of the material being lost in the swamp or shredded by mortars in the retreat. Still, the operation captured the public imagination, and by 994, an influential Anggayan businessman had accumulated a great deal through the use of promethium cables to shore up damaged pipelines.

Basing his reasoning on the long service and resilience of telegraph cables, and aided by extensive undersea surveying undertaken for their maintenance, he was successful in gathering the support of the Ineng and Yeoxli clergy to provide the necessary promethium for a transoceanic conduit. The Yunamer section was completed by 996, and the Ineng section in the following summer. The Arks continued to carry the greater part of transoceanic essence, but are presently considered obsolete following a final expansion of conduit capacity in 1026.
