
Made possible by advancements in the late 990s, the ripple cannon is a multi-charge weapon capable of high-velocity launch using fluid propellant. Each charge is lit synchronously with the passing projectile via a combination of capacitive and arcane measurement, boosting the projectile without waste or risk of rupturing the barrel. Though initially popularised as a means of reducing the barrel wear of high-velocity guns, a particular strength of the system was noted to have been the piecewise ignition of brisant or otherwise marginal propellants, which was soon co-opted by Eiyan military engineers in the pursuit of economic means for long-range bombardment of fortified islands.

For a given weight and cost, ripple cannons do not offer superior single-shot performance to conventional systems; however, their clean combustion means projectiles can be quickly loaded, and the nature of the propellant - liquid oxygen and gelled heavy oil - lends itself to automated injection and mixing. Moreover, the even distribution of pressure means the breech can be made comparatively lightweight, with automated loaders being more easily fitted as a result. For a given fire rate, ripple cannons are far more compact than conventional batteries, making them popular choices when space and manpower are limited.

Due to their bulk and complexity, ripple cannons are typically mounted on trains or barges or assembled on-site, where they can be left unperturbed and continuously supplied from refrigerated tanks of liquid oxygen and heavy oil. Being comparatively front-heavy, their mounts require greater depth than conventional artillery to accommodate the swing of the breech, rendering them unsuitable for field pieces requiring high elevation. Despite this, ripple cannons are still widely employed in the anti-aircraft role: while ineffective against light fighter aircraft, they are critical in deterring bombers and formations from strategic locations.
