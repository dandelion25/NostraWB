Floating lanterns, mentioned in various records since antiquity, have periodically captured the imagination; during festivals, it was a noted in custom in certain states to launch and guide ornate hot air balloons in the shapes of fish and eels. In one notable instance, a disgraced prince was exiled by means of being carried aloft by such a contraption, which washed up on the shore with his corpse in western Miayan. The discovery of hydrogen, a much stronger lifting gas, together with the introduction of the cork lifeboat in the early 800s elevated the role of the balloon from execution device to surveying tool: free-sailing and with controllable altitude, manned balloons were increasingly used to chart the winds and map the earth. Aerodynamic forms were adopted for tethered balloons over the next century, towards the end of which began deployment of the Nexus' first powered dirigibles.

The Siayeo contributed little in the way of early aircraft engines, having invested primarily in engine technologies that were either too cumbersome or unsuited to high-speed operation. Flight experiments were predominantly steam-powered, shifting to imported Maritovan and Interdician internal combustion engines as the technology matured; where the Nexus excelled was the exploration of early aerodynamic and fluid theories, having previously built and maintained extensive experimental facilities using flowing water. Kites, gliders, and aerostats, which were of immediate military interest, benefited significantly from both this accumulated expertise and the presence of long linear acceleration tracks. Their priorities, however, seldom included ambitions of independently operated flight - ultimately, the first true aeroplane would not be of Siayeo make.
