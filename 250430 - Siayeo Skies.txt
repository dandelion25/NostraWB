Floating lanterns, mentioned in various records since antiquity, have periodically captured the imagination; during festivals, it was a noted in custom in certain states to launch and guide ornate hot air balloons in the shapes of fish and eels. In one notable instance, a disgraced prince was exiled by means of being carried aloft by such a contraption, which washed up on the shore with his corpse in western Miayan. The discovery of hydrogen, a much stronger lifting gas, together with the introduction of the cork lifeboat in the early 800s elevated the role of the balloon from execution device to surveying tool: free-sailing and with controllable altitude, manned balloons were increasingly used to chart the winds and map the earth. Aerodynamic forms were adopted for tethered balloons over the next century, towards the end of which began deployment of the Nexus' first powered dirigibles.

The Siayeo contributed little in the way of early aircraft engines, having invested primarily in engine technologies that were either too cumbersome or unsuited to high-speed operation. Flight experiments were predominantly steam-powered, shifting to imported Maritovan, Interdincian, and First Stars internal combustion engines as the technology matured - where the Nexus excelled was the exploration of early aerodynamic and fluid theories, having previously built and maintained extensive experimental facilities using flowing water. Kites, gliders, and aerostats, which were of immediate military interest, benefited significantly from both this accumulated expertise and the presence of long linear acceleration tracks. Their priorities, however, seldom included ambitions of independently operated flight - ultimately, the first true aeroplane would not be of Siayeo make.

While members of the Nexus continued to foster aircraft industries to varying degrees, indigenous winged aircraft development did not begin in earnest until the 1012 demonstration of a flying boat fitted with water-injecting petrol engines. Many noted the high power output achieved through the process, and a renewed interest in steam experiments culminated in the emergence of the pulse combustion monotube boiler in 1019 : a compact, simple, and powerful steam generator with few moving parts and no outside energetic investment. Deafeningly loud and plagued with latent instabilities, it nevertheless paved the way for steam aircraft to become a practical reality - and with the limited size of imported petrol engines, steam turbines became the favoured power plant of projects seeking to transport passengers and cargo across the sea.

Steam condensers for aircraft did not properly develop until 1023, and earlier steam planes were reliant on periodic landings to cool large water reservoirs. Dirigible experiments continued to fuel the development aircraft engines, trying a diverse array of speeds, altitudes, geometries, and navigation techniques in the quest for greater closed-loop power output. The solution was eventually found in the use of wide, flat fuel tanks - typically wing-fitted - as combination radiators, heat exchangers, and reservoirs, with the benefit of preventing heavy fuel oil from freezing in the pipes. Lacking a prominent frontal profile, the new aircraft also benefited from superior aerodynamics at speed, approaching energetic parity with the more thermally efficient internal combustion aircraft; it was at this point that steam aircraft began attracting military attention.

The NT-GS was designed and built in 1026 with the intent of supporting the navy, at a time when QTB fighter forces were untenably underperformant. Using sophisticated optics, photography, radio equipment, and high-altitude flight to spot and track targets across large distances without interception - a task at which it miraculously succeeded, reaching an altitude of 10,000 metres at 210 kph with aid of special breathing equipment. While soon outclassed by Maritovan steam jets in terms of performance, its long range and rugged construction allowed it and its derivatives to remain relevant to the present day, in no small part due to the Siayeo Nexus' cybertronic advantage. Though difficult to maintain at first, serial improvements improved its reliability to the point of its regular use in meteorological and polar survey missions, where its numerous anti-frost measures - a necessity in the humid climate of the equator - are of particular use.

The Anggayan military's favourable experience with the NT-GS paved the way for the commissioning of additional steam aircraft, from medium-sized multirole aircraft to strategic bombers and cargo carriers.
