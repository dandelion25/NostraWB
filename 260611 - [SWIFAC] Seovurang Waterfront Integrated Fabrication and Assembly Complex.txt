**SWIFAC : Seovurang Waterfront Integrated Fabrication and Assembly Complex**
*Whole hulls, no holes.* - SWIFAC Contracting and Procurement, 1027

-- * --


Beginning operation in the year 1023, the Seovurang Waterfront Integrated Fabrication and Assembly Complex (SWIFAC) is a vast shipbuilding complex located in the Shisha port city of Seovurang, under the jurisdiction of Miinsha Capital. Operated jointly by Sxinkanga Shapers, Zigrayig Ordinary Steelworker's Collective, and the Mudena General Company, the complex at peak capacity is capable of putting out to water up to twelve vessels a day with an individual displacement of 2,000~4,000 tonnes. Sustaining this peak capacity is reliant on continuous import of massive amounts of raw material, labour, and finished modular systems, such as integrated boiler-turbine assemblies and electrohydraulic control rooms; though much larger in scale than similar assemblies previously applied to locomotive fabrication, the large shipbuilding demand of Siayeo offshore industries has led the Mudena General Company to create such modules. While SWIFAC primarily focuses on constructing barges, offshore hulls, and bulk haulers, its facilities are often commissioned to lay buoyancy hulls for other vessel classes, including those destined for military use.

Despite having only half the drydock capacity of the second highest capacity shipyard in the Nexus, SWIFAC vastly outstrips it in yearly tonnage by leveraging an advanced forming process developed by Sxinkanga Shapers. Instead of welding or riveting plates individually onto a frame, SWIFAC helically extrudes a continuous sheet of varying thickness using a series of adjustable rolling machines, welds it into an oblong cylindrical blank, and forges it into a double-layered half-hull, incorporating cavities and folded plate structures in lieu of separately installed bulkheads and stiffeners. The two halves are then inspected, finished, outfitted, and then welded together at specially prepared flanges that are welded together using hot hammer-forging methods. Each process is carried out with the help of the facility's 20 abyssal freshwater pools and 844 prometheum hydroblasting dies, developed in collaboration with Velkotran and Kasovatian engineering firms - developed from marine demolition tools, these massive crane-lifted assemblies are capable of introducing large, precise deformations in cold steel in a single application without fracturing the work piece.

Each prometheum hydroblasting die is faced with a set of drilled negative forming blocks of the desired geometry, 10 to 30 centimetres in thickness and cast from a tough alloy of copper, lead, and prometheum that is dimensionally stable and corrosion-resistant. Incorporated into each block is an injection port for oxyliquid explosive, as prescribed by the blast-driven cavity hydroforming (BDCH) process patented by the Zeosuwer Industrial Science Union in Kifia. In lieu of a solid anvil, the BDCH process submerges the work piece deep underwater, then uses a series of directed explosive jets to simultaneously heat the metal and evacuate the forming cavity at a sufficient rate for the ambient hydraulic pressure to accelerate the workpiece into the die; in this way, complex, hollow shapes can be moulded to a high degree of precision using only a single-sided workpiece. Beyond their use in monobloc vessel fabrication, hydroblasting dies have been used extensively in offshore repairs and salvage operations targeting delicate cargo.

Though unmatched in throughput, the profitability of SWIFAC is heavily conditional on the rapid expansion of offshore industries across the Nexus. The concept of a forge-integrated megashipyard for barges had been proposed as early as 968, but the lingering aftershocks of the Nyayanese conquests and the rising instability in Medijan, and the sudden and unexpected collapse of Vernierburg placed a heavy burden on Siayeo efforts to expand their trading fleets. Unable to secure shipbuilding contracts in the necessary volume for SWIFAC, Sxinkanga Shapers turned instead to coastal industries and military contracts - but by 979, Sxinkanga Shapers had already invested significant sums into the operation of 14-metre cylinder assembling machines. While theoretically suitable for boilers and pressure vessels, such large objects could not then be feasibly transported for use on land; they could, however, be repurposed as buoyancy hulls for larger vessels of unconventional make, directly contributing to the revival of uajekals in the late 980s. With the support of both the emergent offshore industry and the timely contribution of massive naval procurement contracts from the rising Velkotran Regency, Sxinkanga Shapers' megashipyard proponents were able to secure the necessary funding and experience to bring SWIFAC technologies to readiness by 1015.
