**Millennium Steel: A New Iron**

The Nexus's demand for fluidic machinery has heavily influenced the course of Siayeo metallurgical industries, tasked with supplying large volumes of strong, abrasion-resistant, dimensionally exact parts. Traditionally, this was achieved through mobilising the massive workforces of the Siayeo religious orders; the early industrialisation of the Nexus was largely enabled by their work, eased by the supply chain developments of the 801 Yunamer Passage Agreement and the exchange rate controls of the 835 Mazbkalawan Trade Convention. As the 900s drew to a close, however, companies from Sxinkanga Shapers to CHARC and the Mudena General Company faced enormous pressure to improve their manufacturing techniques: with the recovery of Maritova, the rise of Nya and Knebonia, and the entry of First Stars into the global market, costly, subsidy-dependent Siayeo craftsmanship was increasingly displaced by cheap foreign parts imported by the emergent class of secular industrial magnates.

In the late 900s, the casting and machining technologies of the Nexus began to reach their limits. Though metallurgy had improved by leaps and bounds with the advent of prometheum pyrometry, the material properties of cast steels were failing to catch up to modern engineering ambitions, and high-strength alloys were too expensive to meet the Nexus' rising demand. Seeking a replacement, Siayeo industry turned first to Maritova: an economy that forged its way over half a world with vast quantities of cheap, strong steel, and leveraged that industry to equip a formidable continental army. Yet Maritova was not the Nexus, and the wholesale import of the Marikovan industrial model would prove unsuccessful: Marikovan-style steel plants had grown to supply one-fourth of the Nexus's structural steel from 980 to 990, but failed to undercut foreign component imports; rather, Siayeo-Marikovan parts had gained a reputation for crudity, unable to match the tolerances to which the Siayeo had become accustomed. To add insult to injury, many such efforts would file bankruptcy come the 990s - Siayeo companies could not establish operations at Marikovan scale without taking on huge debts, and their ability to finance them had been crippled by skyrocketing fuel prices: demand was outstripping the supply of Qayan charcoal, Lhoayan had erupted into civil war, and health-conscious policymakers had placed numerous restrictions on coke production in Yineng.

Cimamo Tangadhui was a prominent mid-900s industrialist, patron and friend to Umadanayan, founder of the Gomotiam School of Economics in Kif. An aesthete and self-dubbed philosopher, he was a proponent of ideal naturalism - or as he considered it, "the inevitable assertion of an enlightened Nature in the spaces we must clear for it." So strongly convinced was he that he uniquely attempted its practice in the manufacture of goods: namely, tin cans, for which he devised numerous systemic production simplifications. Visitors to Cimamo's factory would often marvel at the elegance of the work; bringing an iron sheet to dull red glow, a single press would sprout from it countless impressions fully-formed into cans, which were liberated en masse with a second. Cimamo admired Nya, considering them masters of functional minimalism; he often visited Nya'karazec, and jokingly brought back cheap Nyayanese knock-offs to show off at exhibitions, citing their "enlightening barbarism" and "iridescent frugality". During one such trip in 974, he would pen a series of guides describing his views on 'doing anything worth its sauce'; though derided for their outlandish phrasing and shambolic factuality, Cimamo's treatises would heavily influence the development of Millennium Steel at the turn of the century.

Best known by the promotional work of the Mudena General Company, the Millenium Steel project was an international collaboration between Mudena General - itself a consortium of smaller companies - Xceca Rotary Systems, Tsninah Temple, the Loa Global Armory, and various technical institutions across the Nexus. Underpinned by a carefully negotiated network of contracts and flexible licensing agreements, Millennium Steel set forth to realize a new, fully integrated model of Siayeo steelmaking: not to be discouraged by the previous failure of the Marikovan model, the project would trial the use of increasingly sophisticated, capital-intensive machinery operated on a basis of 'phytonic ownership', as proposed by Umadanayan's 982 treatise *Communion and Consumption: The Sacred Simulacrum*: using reservoir-based accounting, a tree-like symbolic physical process model would be matched to a representation of rapidly buying and selling the asset to arbitrate 'the illusionment of the public good' - akin to an abstracted vending machine. Though this particular licensing experiment lasted only a few years, the technologies developed by Millennium Steel have become indispensable to metallurgical industries across the Nexus.

**Csebi Hydronic Furnace**
(read: Krupp-Renn process / blast furnace hybrid + st e am)
The Csebi hydronic reduction furnace processes charges of coke or charcoal, flux, oil-based additives, and iron ore powder in a tilted horizontal furnace, with charge flow regulated by transverse, bar-shaped oscillating 'gates' actuated via contactless high-temperature inductive prometheum solenoid couplings. The furnace is injected with superheated steam at charge ingress to rapidly develop reducing gases (steam reforming) that react with the ore. On the opposite end, hot blast air is injected into the furnace, raising the temperature and causing the slag-iron complex to partially melt - this mass can be removed and tapped for high-carbon iron, neatly separating the slag without need for further processing. Compared to a traditional blast furnace, the hydronic reduction furnace is easier to monitor, more tolerant of poor-quality feedstock, and comparatively carbon-efficient, saving on fuel and allowing the furnace complexes to be built closer to cities with strict smog laws.

**Draw-Cut-Draw/Extrude/Press (ZTX, or ZTZ/K/C) Solid Casting**
Siayeo ZTX 'solid casting' processes are the result of taking forging, drawing, machining, and combining them to enable the mass production of a wide variety of forms by swapping out different types of high-hardness mandrels and pattern die sets. ZTZ/C begins either with the heating of a billet or, if steel melt is readily available, the extrusion of hot steel into a rough approximating blank using a temperature-resistant prometheum die. The billet is then drawn through a series of water-cooled dies in a steam atmosphere, and cut as needed using an abrasive waterjet fed by a velocity-ram steam pile: a 20-metre tall tower that uses a series of annular steam nozzles to entrain and accelerate a pulsed, thaumically-stilled stream of water downwards into a hydraulic shock ram at 450 m/s (~1-2 kbar). Once the coarse shaping and cutting is complete, the blank is either cold-drawn (if continuous), cold-extruded (if short-segment), and/or die-forged to its final shape - in the latter case, component designers incorporate dimples to allow plastic compensation of volume inconsistencies caused by the roughness of the cutting process. Though ZTX forming requires a great deal more equipment than traditional methods, manufactured components can achieve very high dimensional consistency and production volume owing to the efficacy of the continuous process, the inherent precision of cold working, and the smooth, steam-treated oxide surface.
