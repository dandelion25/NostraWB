**Siayeo Solar Sorcery - Suntiles**

-- * --

"Aürbanisan knows no shadow from dawn til dusk, so bright is the gleam of its suns."
- Miaeftaoram i-Yirde, *Cities of Nanask* (603 AC)

Though their origin is shrouded in mystery, the use of suntiles for redirecting heat and light has been known amongst the peoples of Western Touma since at least the time of Later Nanask (550~720 AC). Possessing the special property of absorbing and releasing rays of sunlight in a directed fashion, they were the basis of early solar concentrators used to cook, cure food, dry laundry and produce high-quality salt prior to the influx of cheaper desert salt from the Kritian Empire, a peculiar measure undertaken by temple workers due to religious restrictions on the burning of 'unclean' woods and coals in sanctified districts. Due to their importance to the temple monopolies, the manufacture of suntiles was kept a closely guarded secret by the clergy prior to the events of the Tiaremed Uprising that ended Nanask. Afterwards, the economic crises of the postwar era, the popularization of cheap coal and the high cost of prometheum continued limited their spread until shortly before the invention of prometheum arc radiators - a conceptual and material descendant of the suntile - in 994, brought about by revival of air purity regulations in the Tiaremed states to combat the respiratory illnesses exacerbated by the high level of toxic contaminants in Siayeo mineral coals.

Suntiles take the appearance of iridescent white-golden plates, formed by carefully setting an acidified mixture of quartz, mercury, and partially refined prometheum flakes at an elevated temperature on an oscillating table, or in the traditional method, manual sloshing above a heated blast over the course of two to seven days. Once formed, the tile can be set in resin or, as more commonly after the year 900, in clear glass. This glass can be formed into durable lenses for more efficient rectangular suntiles, though centrifugal-table based parabolic suntiles remain common for small suntile arrays. Direct grinding and polishing had been performed on suntiles throughout antiquity until the mid-800s, but the release of powderized mercury amalgam and the risk of igniting the prometheum particles made this a hazardous and labour-intensive method that was quickly phased out following the introduction of durable glass sheathing.

A suntile behaves more closely to a luminescent ceramic than a glass mirror. Instead of reflecting light, the suntile material fully absorbs the incoming light through a fluorescent process not yet fully understood - the re-emitted light, negligibly attenuated, bears no image but that of the granular imperfections within, travelling along a radiant path perpendicular to the suntile surface. It hardly need be stated that suntiles are not subject to conventional geometric optics; still, one can achieve their performance using a sufficiently well-adjusted mirror, while suntiles in turn can approximate mirrored geometries with the correct shaping. Because their properties are not interfacial, suntiles are more resistant to damage and fouling than a mirror - performance losses are typically attributable to attenuation of the light over the emission path, rather than compounding surface defects.

With the emergence of affordable prometheum in Siayeo industry from 1000 AC onwards, suntiles have become ubiquitous in the Nexus. Greenhouses, architectural lights, and gas lanterns all rely on suntiles for collection and illumination; despite being functionally supplanted in many ways by the electric light bulb, suntile installations are still retained for their durability, ease of maintenance, and artistic features. Larger suntile arrays, too, are becoming increasingly common, supplying cheap solar heat to residential networks, refineries, stationary pumps, and even unmanned steam batteries - a role for which they are especially suited, directly contributing to the economic superiority of the fireless locomotive for terminal freight in the modern day.

The theory of suntile function is as of yet poorly understood. Suntiles display numerous contradictions of classical optical assumptions, and their thaumic properties remain stubbornly resistant to analysis at currently achievable scales. Nevertheless, there appear to be some early clues pointing towards, according to the venerable and rather infamous thaumiurge Acancangtemu, 'a practical concentration of pure luminosity packed within the form of a musket to incinerate those flying menaces once and for all, just as the whiskered ones intended.' in their treatise, *Nyayanese Myth and the Boundaries of Materia: From Empirical to Theoretical*
