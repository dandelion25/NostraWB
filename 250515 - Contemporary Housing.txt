Siayeo architecture spans a vast swathe of climates and cultures, many of which traditionally rely on unique local resources in the construction of their homes. In more recent times, however, deforestation, urbanisation, and industry have changed the composition of the home to something more commonly recogniseable, differences in layout notwithstanding - in particular, fired brick and mortar buildings have come to dominate many developed areas. Nevertheless, each Siayeo state exhibits a plethora of unique designs to call their own; to demonstrate this, examples of housing are described below.

-- Eiyanixie : Stone Villa

One amidst a row of its ilk, a narrow, two-story building juts out from a hillside. Its stones are fitted loosely, the gaps between filled with bituminous earth and a brown sealant; grains are shaken loose during earthquakes, yet each stone eventually re-settles in its place atop the ones below. The stagger of the stones is arranged into tasteful mosaics, accentuating the ornate carpets draped across the walls; a simple iron chandelier hangs from the ceiling, its incandescent bulbs casting a soft glow across the cemented timbers of the floor. A narrow stairway leads to a cool cellar, dark and lined with smooth concrete; there lies a small fountain, with an ample icebox nearby.
