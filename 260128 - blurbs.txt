Bits & Bobs : Economic Policy
-- --
Each nation of the Siayeo adopts a different economic policy - Loai has a kind of state-supervised corporatist-command economy going on, Urwe is a market economy with indirect but legally strict financial regulations and intervention from the royal treasury, Eiyanixie is legally almost free-market but in practice very static and contract-based, while Shisha is more corpo-hell regulated by competitive clan politics, Anggayan has a large number of medium-size companies with partial state ownership (somewhat like Lhoai, but more flexible), Khifia is a very decentralized economy negotiated between strong local guilds and companies, while Yiwayan and Yineng are market economies with strong central bank influence, with the latter just having much better labour protections. Miayan is somewhat like Urwe, but less legally defined and has more prominent state-owned companies under the aegis of its more authoritative monarchy.

Bits & Bobs : Siayeo temple architecture
-- --
 The Siayeo temples tend to sprawl a bit with their gardens, workshops, and open-air waterworks; while there are many smaller shrines for worship and utility purposes, semi-forested ritual channels and vortex murals spanning multiple acres are still a common sight near cities, taking up much of the area of monasteries and temple complexes that can surpass large towns in population and economic impact. The architecture tends to be very open and interconnected, but shielded from the sun; Siayeo temple works tend to be popular hangouts in summer or the dry season due to the cool breeze they create.
