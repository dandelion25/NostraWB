Short-distance transport in the Nexus is varied, with much of its goods being split between motor trucks, trams, handcarts, and even bicycles as they move to their final destinations.

[scene 1]
The first traction engines were large and heavy, either of imported Maritovan type or built to accommodate modified riverboat boilers. Little changed in this respect after the advent of reservoir engines, used extensively in factory and rail works but generally unsuitable to the sparser, less predictable road.

Passenger trams and canal shuttles of the Nexus have long been at the forefront of indigenous locomotion research. From the first steam engines to pneumatic and electric motors, the complex demands and regulations of the urban environment were quick to be reflected in the engines that serviced them; 

The modern freight tram was born on the streets of Urwe, by way of a series of wide-reaching anti-corruption measures brought to the royal assembly by Nogil Mitansiamoh in 988. In an attempt to streamline taxation via standardized warehousing, low-walled tram cars were commissioned to deliver crates and pallets made to government specifications. Attached to passenger trams, they made regular stops throughout the city; enterprising workers began to offer a kind of delivery service, quietly selling their stock and buying back filled containers at reduced price. The original initiative became mired in controversy, as surveys revealed over-reported sales, missing inventories, and traffic jams caused by loading delays - due to a friendship with the king, however, Nogil was able to adapt and deflect further scrutiny, quietly expanding its scope to include what had already been taking place. The newly christened Medium Carrier Commission, while unable to escape its moniker of 'Nogijebi-ban' (Nogil's peddler department),  inspired similar systems across the Nexus, spurring the development of assisted freight handling and setting the stage for shipping standardisation across the Nexus.
