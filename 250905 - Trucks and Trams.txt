:: **Trucks & tractors**
The first traction engines were large and heavy, being either of imported Maritovan type or of sufficiently heavy construction to accommodate modified stationary engines. Little changed in this respect after the advent of reservoir engines, used extensively in factory and rail works (and motor-driven utility vehicles[!]) but generally unsuitable to the rougher, less predictable circumstances of the road. The transfer of expertise to these more lightweight systems gave tractor designers better high-pressure engines, but automotive steam - particularly condensers - remained closely tied to Maritovan advancements until the fluidic revolution and the arrival of cheap oil in the late 980s.

The 966 Omen Developer precipitated many of the events that we now attribute to the fluidic revolution: the reformation of the temple script, the advent of environmental custodianship, and most crucially in this case, the mobilisation of the vast expertise of the Siayeo Adepts towards the mundane sciences. Compact monotube boilers quickly spread across Siayeo automotive machines around the turn of the century, and were replaced in turn by the thermocoustic pulse combustion boiler, developed in 998 in a collaboration between the Adepts and the Yegorum Technical Institute in Yineng. Suddenly, the main limitation of automotive steam was no longer its bulk and slow startup, but the efficiency of its engine units - once at risk of displacement by foreign diesels, the pulse boiler arrived in time for Nexus's fledgling truck and aircraft industries to exploit the mechanisation of the world's armies.

Though lucky enough to escape much of the chaos that engulfed the world at the turn of the century, observers from Siayeo nations saw the rapid deployment of technologies once unthinkable in the field of war. Whether by the ponderous firepower of Knebonian combat tractors or the fast armored cavalry of the Nyayanese, it was made apparent to the Siayeo that the future lay in mechanization - a lesson also learned much closer to home, with early air power and motorisation playing a key role in the sprawling conflict that embroiled revolutionary Loai. Governments across the Nexus greatly accelerated their investment in both urban and rural transport mechanization, with many establishing driving schools for automobiles in military and government service - in 1025, the Anggayan Postal Service reported that fewer than 5% of their deliveries were made using draft animals.

:: **Trams & tunnels**
Canals have long been at the forefront of Siayeo locomotive research, backed by a long tradition of urban development and public sanitation. Seeking to prevent the pollution of blessed canal waters with animal waste, city officials have sought alternatives to draft animal use since the time of the Yeoxli Empire - while the city of Eiyanixie could command its vastly complex canal system by leveraging hydraulic gates, most Yeoxli and Qayan states relied on ropeways powered by wind and water, quickly adapted to steam engines over the course of the 920s. Yiwayan engineers pioneered increasingly complex cable barge networks throughout the mid-9th century, laying the foundations for centralised traffic control and safety protocols that would quicken the adoption of electricity: Yiwayan would achieve 25% electrification of its urban canals as early as 978, with other nations not far behind. Steam locomotives imported from Maritova were used briefly, but often they were too unwieldy to use alongside the canals crisscrossing Siayeo cities, accumulating significant overheads in bridge construction and foundation repair - it is largely for this reason that they were outcompeted by the much smaller reservoir engines developed from canal mules in the 940s. These in turn saw replacement past the 970s with more efficient, continuously-operating electric locomotives, but the reservoir engines remain where their robustness and superior power are needed.

The urban freight tram was born on the streets of Urwe, by way of a series of wide-reaching anti-corruption measures brought to the royal assembly by Nogil Idanzaboh in 988. In an attempt to standardize private warehousing for improved tax accounting, low-walled tram cars were commissioned to deliver crates and pallets made to government specifications. Attached to passenger trams, they made regular stops throughout the city; enterprising workers began to offer a kind of delivery service, quietly selling their stock and buying back filled containers at reduced price. The original initiative became mired in controversy, as surveys revealed over-reported sales, missing inventories, and traffic jams caused by loading delays - due to a friendship with the king, however, Nogil was given leeway to adapt and deflect further scrutiny, quietly expanding its scope to include what had already been taking place. The newly christened Medium Carrier Commission, while unable to escape its moniker of 'Nogijabanbur' (Nogil's peddler department), inspired similar systems across the Nexus, setting the stage for the transnational mail standardisation initiatives of the late 1010s.
