bits and bobs: Terra Nectere

1. There are regions in the Nexus where hydrological assimilation has progressed to the point that even falling rain carries the Great Tree's influence. Dew, rain, and mist-catching magical devices are developed for use in these regions, and skilled adepts can tap into the Great Tree's awareness to monitor and perturb their climate. Terraforming initiatives have significant contributions to the Nexus's current prosperity - more than half of its fresh water is supplied from artificial microclimate regions.

2. The magic performed by the effigies draws from the greater awareness of the Great Tree, and relies on the manipulation of water and steam. In addition to piloting the effigies, recorded uses include clearing clogged waterways, and collecting fish, among other things; it is generally understood that it is employed more subtly in carrying out the will of the Great Tree.

3. Lands assimilated by the Great Tree tend to become more productive over time, with measurable increases in soil quality, water quality, and forest health; with the development of thaumic computers in Shisha, this has been extended to more abstract tasks involving flows, such as market management, traffic control, and power distribution. The prioritisation of this technology by Shisha has elevated it to its current position of power, serving as the computational capital of the Nexus.

4. The 'gift of harmony' describes a belief of the Lotus Cult, which casts the Great Tree's continued expansion as a means of achieving enlightenment and oneness with the world. Their funerary rites involve casting the ashes of the dead into sanctified streams and rivers; adherents who reach greater heights of personal and environmental harmony are thought to be able to join the Great Tree and become more enlightened than their physical embodiments allow.

Meteorological and Transpiration Considerations for Tree-Based Magical Influence

The Tree's aboveground magical influence is intimately linked to hydrological conditions. Its power relies on the presence of water as a conductive medium, with soil moisture, surface water, and atmospheric humidity acting as natural conduits. Rainfall, snowmelt, and local aquifers become strategic variables: sufficient precipitation enhances the Tree's ability to project its influence, while droughts or dry spells weaken it, limiting the effectiveness of aboveground magical operations.

Operationally, this means that military or magical deployments dependent on the Tree's influence are contingent on the hydrological state. Rainy periods effectively open windows of opportunity for deploying forces or activating aboveground nodes, whereas dry periods constrain reach, akin to impassable terrain in conventional operations.

Forests naturally augment this effect through evapotranspiration. According to the US Forest Service, an acre of mature forest can transpire approximately 4,000 gallons (about 15,000 liters) of water per day during peak growth periods. Rainwater collection and surface runoff in forested regions further contribute to the available medium: local small ponds, streams, and puddles in temperate forests can account for several thousand liters per hectare after a moderate rain (~20-50 mm), while larger catchments in tropical forests can exceed 100,000 liters per hectare.

Strategically, understanding local precipitation patterns and forested hydrology becomes essential. Operations in areas with consistent rainfall or dense forest coverage maximize the Tree's aboveground influence, while arid regions or periods of drought reduce efficacy, requiring pre-positioned conduits, totems, or alternative nodes to sustain magical operations.
