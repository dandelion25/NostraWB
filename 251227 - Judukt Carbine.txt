**Judukt Carbine: A Volley for Cheap**

-- -- (inspiration: Belton flintlock)

First created in the mid-9th century by associates of an Nanakay smuggling cartel, the chain-firing 'Judukt' carbine was rightly regarded with contempt by formal militaries. It was unconventional, poorly manufactured, prone to misfires, and inaccurate due to the varying barrel length of each shot. Nevertheless, it was still a cheap repeater capable of firing full-power ammunition with almost no moving parts, resulting in its rapid adoption by gangs, partisans, pirates, and militia forces across the Nexus. Over a million Judukts of varying make are estimated to have been built and sold over the course of the late 9th century, emerging in skirmishes and civil wars across the world. Though perhaps overshadowed by Nyayanese contributions, the emergence of the Judukt carbine is thought to have significantly hastened the adoption of automatic rifles by militaries foreign and domestic.

The Judukt carbine is loaded using a long tube capped at one end, serving as a combination breech and magazine. Each tube carries a bonded 'stick' of alternating propellant (an uncased wad of propellant, glue, and binder of varying composition) and projectile sections, with more sophisticated examples including thin sealing disks between rounds to limit unintended chain-fire. Semi-automatic fire is achieved by indexing and engaging a disc of coded electrical contacts with each trigger pull, causing a battery(zinc-carbon dry cell)-charged Leyden jar to spark at the correct location and ignite the propellant. Each rifle comes with a short push rod with which failed rounds can be protruded and broken free from the tube magazine; however, it is common for fighters using poor quality ammunition to abandon misfired tubes for fear of a sudden detonation.
