450 AC : The Four Corners and Eight Banners
-- --

The final disintegration of Yeoxli between 400-430, already long enfeebled, marked a turning point for the region's politics - kingdoms that had for centuries given tribute and vassalage, now found themselves bereft of the diplomatic buffer that had anchored factions and stayed minor conflicts across nearly half a continent. Power struggles intensified within cities, towns, and villages, throwing into disarray what little of the tax system that did not fall prey to corruption; tolls changed from week to week, and the rivers of trade - of tin, copper, lead, clay, paper, grain, salt, medicine, and currency - shrivelled. What could not be bought was now taken, by grift, by law, by force, and by neglect: from the neighbours, the palaces, and even the waterworks, in an ongoing process that strained the nascent Siayeo Nexus.

The Adepts, and indeed the Great Tree itself, were nothing if not resourceful. Already there was improvement over the degrading conduits of Yeoxli, with the emergence of 'whorls' - swirling bodies that slowed the dispersion of essence - amidst other, more direct efforts to secure the Root, many with the aim of impressing local lords. Moreover, as traditional waterworks declined, many fields in the coastal lowlands grew laden with salt - but at some point the Great Tree, having received graftings and prayers from distant pilgrims, began to purify the soil. Such miracles contributed to the continued growth of the Siayeo, even in these troubled times.

By the year 600, three states would emerge dominant in the region, two of them Yeoxli - and much of it would be shaped by echoes of the empire. Yet to tell the whole story, most historians agree that one must look both back and beyond, in the lands across the Yunamer Passage.
